# Finding: Scintigraphy for Gastrointestinal Bleeding

**Source section:** GASTROINTESTINAL HEMORRHAGE
**Quality score:** 0.4
**Differential count:** 9

## Differentials

- Rate of hemorrhage ◊  If bleeding not detectable by RBC scintigraphy, it will not be detectable by angiography!
  - RBC scan detects bleeding as low as 0.1 mL/min
  - Catheter angiography requires bleeding rates of ≥ 0.5 mL/min:
- Continuous versus intermittent bleeding: most GI hemorrhages are intermittent
- Site of hemorrhage in reference to specific vascular territory: eg, celiac, superior mesenteric, inferior mesenteric artery
- Characteristics of radionuclide agent 99mTc-Labeled Autologous RBCs (In Vivtro Labeling Preferred) ◊  Generally preferred and accepted most sensitive imaging method for lower GI bleeding ◊  Serves to triage patients for angiography as a negative exam predicts a negative arteriogram Indications:   acute / intermittent arterial or venous bleeding; NOT useful in occult bleeding
- Esophagus
- Duodenum
- Small bowel
- Meckel diverticulum
- Duplication cyst

## Context

Detection depends on: 63% (39%) sensitive for upper (lower) GI bleed ◊  Only 50% of angiograms will be positive after a positive scintigram! ◊  A positive scintigram increases likelihood of a positive angiogram from 22% to 53%! ◊  A hemodynamically unstable patient with systolic pressures < 100 mmHg should go to angiography!
