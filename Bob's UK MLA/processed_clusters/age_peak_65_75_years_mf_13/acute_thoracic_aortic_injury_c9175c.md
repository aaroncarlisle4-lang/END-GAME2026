# Finding: Acute Thoracic Aortic Injury

**Source section:** Age peak: 65-75 years; M÷F = 1÷3
**Quality score:** 1.0
**Differential count:** 18

## Differentials

- severe chest pain: precordial (ascending aorta), neck-jaw (aortic arch), interscapular (descending thoracic aorta)
  - anterior chest wall contusion, dyspnea, dysphagia
  - blood pressure changes:
  - unexplained hypotension
  - scapulothoracic syndrome = decreased / absent upper extremity pulses
  - acute coarctation syndrome = decreased / absent lower extremity + normal upper extremity pulses with upper extremity hypertension + systolic murmur in 2nd left parasternal interspace
- Bronchus depression (left main)
- Aortic silhouette shaggy
- Death in 80-90%
- Mediastinal widening
- Enteric (nasogastric) tube displacement
- Apical cap
- Tracheal shift
- Normal well-defined mediastinal + aortic contours on CXR: no further imaging
- Stable patient:
- Unstable patient + unequivocally abnormal CXR / strong clinical evidence of aortic injury:
- Antihypertensive medication
- Stent graft for high-risk patients
- Surgical repair : 20-54% mortality, 5-10% morbidity ← paraplegia
- 80-90% fatal at scene of accident ◊  Mortality rises rapidly within first 24 hours
- 10-20% reach hospital ← formation of periaortic hematoma + false aneurysm contained by adventitia ± surrounding connective tissue (a)  without intervention: 30% (40-50%) dead within 6 (24) hours; 90% dead within 4 months; chronic false aneurysm may develop in 2-5% at isthmus / descending aorta (b)  with surgical repair: 60-70% survive; surgical mortality rate of 9-44% varies with degree of hemodynamic instability + severity of associated injuries + magnitude of aortic laceration
- Chronic pseudoaneurysm (1%): potentially unstable
- symptom-free period of months to years (in 11% > 10 years)
  - delayed clinical symptoms: 42% (85%) within 5 (20) years consisting of chest pain, back pain, dyspnea, cough, hoarseness, dysphagia, systolic murmur Location:  descending aorta at level of lig. arteriosum filling the aorticopulmonary window (most commonly)
