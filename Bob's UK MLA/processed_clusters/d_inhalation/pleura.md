# Finding: PLEURA

**Source section:** D. INHALATION
**Quality score:** 0.7
**Differential count:** 22

## Differentials

- pleuritic back / shoulder pain, dyspnea (in 80-90%)
- Trauma
- Honeycomb lung, Hamman-Rich syndrome
- Emphysema, Esophageal rupture
- Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
- Hyaline membrane disease
- Endometriosis
- Spontaneous, Scleroderma
- Tuberous sclerosis
- Sarcoma (osteo-), Sarcoidosis
- Eosinophilic granuloma
- Tuberculosis + fungus
- Closed pneumothorax = intact thoracic cage
- Open pneumothorax = "sucking" chest wound
- Tension pneumothorax (clinical diagnosis) =  accumulation of air within pleural space ← free ingress + limited egress of air
- compromised venous return
- Tension hydropneumothorax
- Anteromedial pneumothorax (earliest location)
- Subpulmonic / anterolateral pneumothorax (2nd most common location)
- Apicolateral pneumothorax (least common location)
- Posteromedial pneumothorax (in presence of lower lobe collapse)
- Pneumothorax → outlines pulmonary ligament Prognosis:  resorption of pneumothorax occurs at a rate of 1.25% per day (accelerated by increasing inspired oxygen concentrations)

## Context

Pneumothorax =  accumulation of air within pleural space Pathophysiology:   disruption of visceral pleura / trauma to parietal pleura Cause: Types: Pathophysiology: intrapleural pressure exceeds atmospheric pressure in lung during expiration (check-valve mechanism) Frequency:   in 3-5% of patients with spontaneous pneumothorax, higher in barotrauma √  hyperexpanded ipsilateral chest √  mediastinal shift to contralateral side √  contralateral displacement of anterior junction line √  "deep sulcus" sign = on frontal view larger lateral costodiaphragmatic recess than on opposite side √  flattenin
