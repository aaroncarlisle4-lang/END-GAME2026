STREP ABC

Chest — Upper lobe fibrosis
Upper lobe pulmonary fibrosis has a significantly more restricted differential diagnosis compared to lower lobe fibrosis
S
Sarcoidosis: bilateral hilar adenopathy with upper zone fibrosis[1]
Key: perilymphatic nodules along fissures and bronchovascular bundles
T
Tuberculosis: upper lobe consolidation with cavitation and fibrosis[2]
Key: centrilobular tree-in-bud nodules indicating active endobronchial
R
Radiation: paramediastinal fibrosis with sharp geometric straight margins[3]
Key: sharp non-anatomical borders conforming to radiation portals
E
Extrinsic allergic alveolitis: centrilobular nodules with ground-glass and mosaic attenuation[4]
Key: headcheese pattern with expiratory lobular air trapping
P
Pneumoconiosis: conglomerate upper lobe masses with background nodularity[5]
Key: progressive massive fibrosis migrating towards the hila
A
Ankylosing spondylitis: apical fibrobullous disease with upward hilar retraction[6]
Key: coexistent bamboo spine and bilateral symmetrical sacroiliitis
B
Berylliosis: reticulonodular opacities radiologically identical to pulmonary sarcoidosis
Key: occupational aerospace or high-tech metal exposure history
C
Cystic fibrosis: upper lobe predominant cystic and cylindrical bronchiectasis[7]
Key: extensive mucoid impaction and hyperinflation in youth
Clinical Pearl Ankylosing spondylitis is the 'odd one out' among connective tissue diseases; it causes upper lobe fibrosis, whereas rheumatoid arthritis and scleroderma characteristically cause lower lobe fibrosis[8].
